A list beats a memory for a reason that has nothing to do with intelligence. Working memory is small, it degrades under stress, and it is easily crowded out by the most urgent thing in the room, which is rarely the most important thing. A written step does not panic. When Pronovost et al. (2006) introduced a five-item checklist for inserting central lines in intensive-care units, the items were not news to any physician on the ward. Wash your hands. Clean the skin. Use full barrier precautions. The result was a collapse in infection rates that held for months, not because anyone learned something new, but because the knowing was finally converted into doing every single time.

That is the mechanism worth naming. A checklist externalizes the steps that expertise tends to skip, and it distributes the authority to stop. The junior nurse empowered to halt a senior physician who reaches for the catheter without washing is doing more for safety than another year of the physician's training would add. The list is only the visible artifact. The real change is a small redistribution of who is allowed to say wait. Organizations that adopt the form without the redistribution get the paperwork and none of the protection, and then they conclude, wrongly, that checklists do not work.
