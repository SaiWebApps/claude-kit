Every high-stakes profession eventually rediscovers the checklist, and every time it does, someone objects that a list is beneath the expertise of the people asked to use it. The objection has the logic exactly backward. A checklist is not a substitute for skill. It is insurance against the predictable ways that skill fails under load. Surgeons do not forget how to operate. They forget, on the four-hundredth procedure of a long month, to confirm the patient's name or to give the antibiotic on time, because the routine steps are precisely the ones attention stops guarding. Gawande (2009) built an entire argument for medicine around this gap between knowing what to do and reliably doing it, and the argument travels well beyond the operating room.

Consider the two failure modes an expert actually faces. The first is ignorance, not knowing the right thing to do. The second is ineptitude, knowing the right thing and failing to do it. Training, credentialing, and experience attack the first. They do almost nothing for the second, and in complex work the second is now the larger share of the damage. Yet most organizations keep investing as though ignorance were the whole problem. They hire for more expertise and wonder why avoidable errors persist. The checklist is cheap precisely because it targets the neglected half, the doing rather than the knowing, and it is unglamorous for the same reason.

The deeper objection is about autonomy, the sense that a list treats a professional like a machine to be programmed. Yet the opposite is true. By offloading the handful of steps that are known, routine, and easy to drop, a checklist frees attention for the parts of the work that genuinely require judgment. It automates the bottom of the task so the expert can spend more of themselves at the top of it. That is not a demotion of expertise. It is a promotion of it, and the best practitioners tend to be the ones who accept the trade first.
