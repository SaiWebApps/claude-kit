None of this means a checklist is free, or that more items make a better list. A checklist that grows without discipline becomes theater, a form that gets signed rather than a step that gets done, and a signed form is worse than nothing because it manufactures the appearance of care. The discipline is to keep the list short, to include only the steps that are both consequential and genuinely skippable, and to revise it whenever reality argues back. A good list is edited more often than it is obeyed.

There is also a class of failure a checklist cannot reach. In his study of catastrophic breakdowns in tightly coupled systems (Perrow, 1984), accidents emerge from the interaction of many small, individually reasonable decisions, and no pre-written list anticipates them because the danger lives in the combination, not in any single step. For that, an organization needs slack, diverse judgment, and the freedom to stop the line. The checklist handles the predictable, and it handles it beautifully. The harder work, the work no form will do for you, is staying honest about everything the list leaves out.
